About HBV

Hepatitis B is an infectious disease affecting the liver caused by the Hepatitis B Virus (HBV). HBV can cause either an acute or chronic infection of Hepatitis B. While many individuals do not immediately develop symptoms upon contracting the virus, acute symptoms that can last up to several weeks include yellowing of the skin and eyes (jaundice), dark urine, extreme fatigue, nausea, vomiting and abdominal pain (1). In a small percentage of individuals, the disease can progress to the chronic phase, causing liver cirrhosis (scarring), liver cancer, or acute liver failure, all of which can lead to death (1). In 2010, about 786,000 deaths could be attributed to hepatitis B: 132,200 due to acute hepatitis B liver failure, 341,400 due to hepatitis B-caused liver cancer, 312,400 due to cirrhosis induced by hepatitis B (2). The World Health Organization (WHO) estimates that the number of deaths due to hepatitis B has increased to 887,000 in 2015 (1). The numbers indicate the severity of hepatitis B and the importance that the world must place on this major global health problem. One recent study indicates that the current prevalence of worldwide acute hepatitis B is around 168 million while the prevalence of global chronic hepatitis B infection is close to 400 million cases (3). These numbers include carriers of the disease who have HBV but do not exhibit symptoms. While acute infection of hepatitis B in adults leads to chronic hepatitis B in less than 5% of cases, acute infection of hepatitis B in infants and young children leads to chronic hepatitis B in around 95% of cases (1). HBV is transmitted via blood or semen through processes such as sexual contact, intravenous contact, and perinatally from infected mother to neonate (4). While there are treatments for chronic hepatitis, there remains no cure. These treatments are frequently long-term to suppress the effects of chronic hepatitis B. There are currently 7 approved treatments for hepatitis B (5). Despite there being no cure, an effective vaccine has been available since the 1980s (6). Post-exposure prophylaxis, the prevention of infection after exposure, is also possible with the vaccine or a combination of hepatitis B vaccination and hepatitis B immunoglobulin (5). This process is effective, safe, and indicated in many possible routes of HBV exposure, including for newborns born to hepatitis B positive mothers, sexual exposure, or after liver transplantation (6).

HBV and the Chinese American Community

Prevalence of chronic HBV varies greatly across countries. Prior to the expansion of the vaccine, the World Health Organization categorized 37 countries, including China, into the Western Pacific Region, which was disproportionately impacted by hepatitis B; despite having 28% of the world’s population, this region accounted for over 50% of all deaths caused by hepatitis B (7). In 1992, a large national survey of over 61,000 Chinese citizens found that 9.75% of subjects tested positive for hepatitis B surface antigen (HBsAg), an indicator for positive hepatitis B infection (8). Despite the demonstrable effect of the hepatitis B vaccine, HBV still remains a big issue in Chinese American communities today. One study conducted in Chicago’s Chinatown found HBV prevalence in 11.1% of patients presenting for general medical care.(9) Various studies conducted of Chinese-speaking and Chinese immigrant populations across North America have found that screening rates vary between 33% - 57% and vaccination rates are between 31% - 38% (10–12). It is crucial to visit your doctor or a suitable clinic to be tested and vaccinated. Please check our “Useful Links” page to see locations where you and your children can be screened and vaccinated.
